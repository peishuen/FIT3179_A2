Resilient Malaysia

The Economic Journey Before, During and After COVID-19

From Slowdown to Strong Comeback

Malaysia’s economy slowed slightly from 2017 to 2019 as global demand softened, setting the stage before the pandemic. When lockdowns began in 2020, activity collapsed, causing the first GDP contraction in over two decades. The strong rebound that followed, driven by exports and reopening, highlights Malaysia’s resilience and ability to recover from the pandemic shock.

Jobs Lost, Then Slowly Regained

Malaysia’s unemployment rate stayed low and stable before 2020, supported by steady growth and domestic demand. The pandemic shock triggered sudden job losses, especially in services and tourism, lifting unemployment to its highest level in decades. As restrictions eased, policy support and rehiring gradually restored jobs, highlighting the labour market’s slow but resilient recovery.

Uneven Impacts Across the Nation

During 2020–2021, unemployment rose unevenly across Malaysia, exposing clear regional differences in impact. Smaller and less diversified states, such as Sabah, Kelantan, and Perlis, recorded higher job losses, while urban and industrial areas weathered the shock more effectively. This reveals how differing economic structures shaped resilience and vulnerability across regions.

Widening Gaps in State Recovery

The gap between 2019 and 2023 growth rates reveals how recovery varied sharply across states. While a few states, including Johor and Pahang, managed to surpass pre-pandemic levels, most still lag behind, weighed down by slower trade and investment momentum. This highlights persistent growth gaps shaped by differing economic structures and recovery capacities.

A Shift in What Became Expensive

From 2019 to 2023, pandemic shifts and reopening reshaped Malaysia’s inflation trends. Restaurants and accommodation rose sharply as demand rebounded, while health costs stayed elevated with ongoing medical needs. Meanwhile, housing and utilities eased in rank as subsidies and stable rents kept prices contained. This highlights how post-COVID recovery changed the main drivers of living costs.

Trade Flows Interrupted, Then Revived

Malaysia’s exports and imports both declined sharply in 2020 as border closures and supply-chain disruptions stalled global trade flows. From 2021 onward, demand recovery, industrial reopening, and stronger electronics exports reignited trade activity, lifting it well above pre-pandemic levels. This highlights Malaysia’s strong trade resilience and continued dependence on global market revival.
